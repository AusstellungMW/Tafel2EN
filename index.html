The Liberation from National Socialism

"The coming night will bring us undisturbed sleep after days of tension."
A resident of Wolfenbüttel described the overwhelming sense of relief that many in the city felt on the day the war ended (Detlef Endeward, Fritz Mauss, and Joachim Schlüchtermann, Wolfenbüttel after ’45: A City Tells Its Postwar Story, published by the State Center for Political Education, Hanover, 1986, p. 8).

The deep rumble of engines and the unmistakable sound of tracked vehicles—the characteristic noise of the Sherman tanks of the U.S. 9th Army—heralded the imminent collapse of National Socialist rule in Wolfenbüttel. On Wednesday, April 11, 1945, World War II came to an end for the city’s civilian population. In the days leading up to this event, Hartmann Lauterbacher, the Nazi Gauleiter of South Hanover-Brunswick, had filled the front page of the Braunschweiger Tageszeitung (Wolfenbüttel edition), which continued to be published until April 10, with threats against anyone who refused to resist. One of the brutal atrocities of the war’s final days included the SS hanging deserters from trees as a deterrent. Another grim indication of the desperation of the Nazi regime was the publication in the newspaper of detailed instructions on how to use a Panzerfaust (anti-tank weapon), calling on Hitler Youth members, Volkssturm militia, and even civilians to halt the advance of Allied tank units. From the west, U.S. combat units approached the outskirts of Wolfenbüttel, consisting of infantry, tanks, and reconnaissance vehicles. Having encountered resistance elsewhere in their advance, the troops proceeded with extreme caution—only to find none. By 12:30 p.m., Allied forces had secured all key locations and major streets in the city. In the hours before their arrival, the residents of Wolfenbüttel had hung white flags from their windows, signaling their willingness to surrender. Yet, despite this act of submission, fear lingered. Many elderly people, German deserters, and mothers with children continued to hide in their cellars, terrified that the occupying forces might exact brutal revenge upon the civilian population.
